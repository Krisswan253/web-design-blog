Reading 2

  • 04 March 2026

Response

After reading about invisible infrastructure in the previous texts, this set of readings felt like a continuation of that idea, but focused more on platforms and data rather than physical systems. If the earlier readings made the internet feel more grounded and material, these ones make it clear how much control exists on top of that infrastructure, even if we do not see it.

The platform reading focuses on how dominant tech companies act as gatekeepers. They are not just hosting content but structuring access to entire systems. As the text explains, “a handful of digital platforms mediate a growing share of online commerce and communications” (Separation of Platforms and Commerce). That line made it clear how concentrated this power actually is. These platforms are not just tools people use. They are environments that shape visibility, distribution, and opportunity.

The Markup article about Facebook makes that control feel even more concrete. Even though Facebook publicly removed racial ad targeting categories, the investigation found that advertisers could still reach specific groups through indirect methods. As the article explains, there are “a wide array of proxies” that allow advertisers to target users without explicitly naming race (Facebook Got Rid of Racial Ad Categories. Or Did It?). What stood out to me here is how similar this feels to the idea of invisible infrastructure. The system appears to change on the surface, but underneath it continues to function in almost the same way.

What connects these readings to the previous ones is this idea that both infrastructure and platforms operate in ways that are hidden from users. Before, that invisibility showed up in physical systems like undersea cables and data centers. Here, it shows up in algorithms, categories, and targeting systems. In both cases, the lack of visibility makes it harder to question how these systems work or who controls them.

Reflection

Thinking about these readings together makes the internet feel a lot less neutral. On one level, there is the physical infrastructure that carries data, which is already uneven and hidden. On top of that, there are platforms that organize and interpret that data, deciding what people see and how they are categorized. Both layers operate in ways that are not immediately visible, but they shape experience in really significant ways.

It also made me think more critically about the idea of the “cloud” that came up in the previous readings. If the cloud hides the physical side of the internet, platforms hide the social and political side. Together, they create this sense that everything is seamless and natural, when in reality it is highly structured and controlled.

What feels most unsettling is how subtle all of this is. It does not feel like control when you are using these platforms. It just feels like scrolling or browsing. But behind that, there are systems constantly sorting, predicting, and shaping what you see. Even when changes are made, like removing certain targeting categories, the underlying logic can still remain.

Overall, connecting these readings makes it clear that invisibility is not just a feature of infrastructure, but a core part of how the internet functions. Whether it is cables under the ocean or algorithms deciding what content appears, the most powerful parts of the system are often the least visible.
