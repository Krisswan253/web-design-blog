## Response

These readings made me think about the internet in a much more physical way than I usually do. I feel like I am so used to thinking of it as something abstract or invisible, like data just moving through space, that I forget how much actual infrastructure is involved. What stood out across both readings is how much of that infrastructure is intentionally hidden, and how that invisibility shapes the way we understand and interact with technology.

In *How to See Invisible Infrastructure*, the idea that infrastructure becomes visible when it breaks or fails really stuck with me. It made me realize how rarely we actually think about the systems that support everyday life unless something goes wrong. There is this assumption that everything just works, and that assumption depends on the fact that most of the system stays out of sight. Once you start looking for it, it becomes clear how much labor and maintenance is constantly happening in the background, even though it is almost never acknowledged.

*The Undersea Network* takes that idea further by focusing on the physical cables that carry most internet traffic across the ocean. One line that stood out was the idea that we should look “down, rather than up to the sky” to understand network infrastructure (The Undersea Network). That completely shifts how the internet is usually imagined. Instead of something floating in a cloud, it becomes something grounded, routed, and tied to specific locations. The fact that nearly all transoceanic data moves through these cables makes the system feel a lot more uneven and vulnerable than it is usually presented.

What I kept thinking about while reading this is how misleading the idea of the “cloud” actually is. Companies like Apple make it feel like our data exists in this clean, invisible space, when in reality it is stored in physical locations and moved through very real systems. That disconnect makes it easier not to question where data actually goes, who has access to it, and how it is controlled. The infrastructure is hidden, but that also means the power behind it is less visible.

## Reflection

What I keep coming back to is how much invisibility shapes our understanding of technology. If we do not see the systems, we are less likely to question them. These readings made me realize that the internet is not just hidden by accident, but that its invisibility plays a role in how it operates socially and politically.

At the same time, thinking about the physical side of networks makes them feel more fragile. The idea that global communication depends on cables running across the ocean floor makes the system feel less stable than it is usually portrayed. It also makes it easier to imagine how disruptions could have large effects, even if we do not normally think about that.

More than anything, these readings made me more aware of how constructed the internet actually is. It is not just a neutral space where information flows freely. It is built, maintained, and controlled in specific ways, even if those processes are not visible to most users. Once you start thinking about it like that, it becomes harder to ignore how much of the system operates outside of what we can see or access.
