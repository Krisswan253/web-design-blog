## Response

Reading these texts together made me rethink the internet in a way I was not really expecting. Instead of seeing it as something fixed or just “how things are,” it started to feel more like something that is constantly being shaped by the people who use it. Across all three readings, what stood out most was the idea that networks are not neutral. They reflect certain values, and they could just as easily be built differently.

In *Another Network is Possible*, the internet is framed less as a technical system and more as something social and political. The reading pushes back on the idea that huge platforms are just the natural outcome of technology. Instead, it suggests that networks could be built around completely different priorities, like care or cooperation. The line “another network is possible” (Another Network is Possible) really stuck with me because it makes the current version of the internet feel less inevitable. It starts to feel like a choice rather than the only option.

*Architecting Emergence* was probably the reading I connected with the most. I liked how it focuses on designing systems that allow things to grow and change instead of controlling them. The idea that designers should create environments where “emergence can take place” (Architecting Emergence) feels very different from the kind of rigid, efficiency-driven systems we usually see online. It actually reminded me a lot of an environmentalist mindset, where you are not trying to control every outcome but instead create conditions where ecosystems can sustain themselves. As someone who is more interested and minoring in environmental studies, I am always paying attention in how to connect environmental studies and the modern day interne. This approach felt more intuitive and even more ethical. It shifts design away from control and toward something more relational and responsive.

The article about the woman who taught strangers online to care for one another made everything feel more grounded. What stood out here was that the platform itself was not really built for that kind of interaction, but people still created it anyway. That shows how much of the internet is shaped by users, not just by the systems themselves. At the same time, it also highlights how much effort it takes to create spaces that actually feel supportive when the platform does not naturally encourage that kind of behavior.

## Reflection

What I keep thinking about is how different the internet could feel if it were built around other values. Right now, so much of it is based on visibility and speed, which can make everything feel kind of surface level. These readings point toward something slower and more intentional, where connection actually matters. That kind of shift feels hard to imagine at a large scale, but it also feels necessary.

It also made me think differently about design. I usually think of design as something visual, but these readings make it clear that it shapes how people interact. The way something is structured can push people toward certain behaviors, whether that is competition, isolation, or care. That makes design feel less like just making something look good and more like shaping how people relate to each other.

There is also something interesting about how these alternative networks already exist, just on a smaller scale. They are not dominant, but they are still meaningful. It makes me wonder if change comes from building completely new systems or from slowly shifting the ones we already use.

Overall, these readings made me realize that the internet is not as fixed as it seems. There are other ways it could be structured, and some of those possibilities are already starting to show up in smaller, more intentional communities. Thinking about it through both a design lens and a more environmental way of thinking makes those possibilities feel more real and worth paying attention to.
